# Offer Analysis and Recommendation — Passatge d'Alió (ref. ONB2602004)

## Executive summary

The property is listed at **€495,000**. My recommendation is to make a **firm offer of €435,000** (12% below asking), with a realistic goal of **closing between €450,000 and €460,000**. The reasoning: the price per square metre is high for the area and, above all, this is the **last unit in the development**, which puts time and selling pressure on the buyer's side.

---

## The property

- **Location:** Passatge d'Alió, next to Passeig de Sant Joan (Dreta de l'Eixample, Barcelona) — one of the most sought-after areas of the city.
- **Type:** comprehensive rehabilitation of an 1884 building (original façade, beams and stone walls preserved). Not ground-up new construction, but a high-quality renovation.
- **Features:** 54 m², furniture included, high-end finishes.
- **Development:** only 5 homes in total; **this is the last one available**.
- **Time on market:** approximately **two months**.

---

## Why €435,000 is a reasonable offer (not a lowball)

### 1. The price per m² is above market

- €495,000 ÷ 54 m² = **€9,170/m²**.
- **Asking** prices in Dreta de l'Eixample: ~€8,200–8,800/m² (this unit already starts above the high end).
- **Actual registered closing** prices in the area: ~€6,700/m².
- Even accepting a reasonable premium for the renovation, quality, and furniture, **€9,170/m² is expensive**. After dropping to €435,000, you'd still pay ~€8,060/m², still at the top.

### 2. It's the last unit → maximum negotiating leverage

In new-build and renovated developments, **the fewer homes remain, the more room the developer gives**: they want to sell 100% to close out the development, free up financing, and stop carrying the unit. That it's the last one — and the smallest (often why it's last) — strengthens our position.

### 3. It's been unsold for two months

Not an extreme-urgency situation (premium product in Barcelona can take 3–6 months), but **two months on the last unit confirms the pressure is on the seller's side, not the buyer's**. We can afford to wait; they increasingly can't.

### 4. We are a strong buyer

- Pre-approved financing.
- Ability to complete quickly.
- This is highly valuable to a developer who wants to close: a fast, certain buyer is worth more than waiting for an uncertain higher bidder.

## Tax point to confirm (important for total cost)

Since this is a **rehabilitation**, you must confirm with the agent how the sale is taxed, because it changes the final cost:

- **VAT (10%) + AJD (1.5%)** → typical for new construction / first transfer after comprehensive rehabilitation.
- **ITP (~10–11% in Catalonia)** → if treated as a second transfer.

**Exact question for the agent:** *"Does the sale pay VAT + AJD, or ITP?"*

> On a close-out unit like this, the developer may even be willing to **absorb that tax** as a way of conceding without dropping the advertised price. Worth putting on the table.

## Present-day cost of the purchase (present value, 25 years)

Even though selling recovers the property's value, the purchase has a real economic cost. Assuming a **25-year fully amortized mortgage** and selling the home after 25 years:

**Assumptions:**

- Purchase price: €455,000 (expected close)
- Down payment (20%): €91,000 · Mortgage: €364,000 over 25 years, fixed 3%
- Purchase taxes and fees: 13% = €59,150
- Ownership costs: ~€5,000/yr (IBI, community fees, insurance, maintenance)
- Selling costs: ~4% · Inflation: 2% · Real discount rate: 2%

**If sold at the same real value (no appreciation):**

| Item | Present value |
|---|---|
| Down payment | +€91,000 |
| Purchase taxes and fees | +€59,150 |
| Mortgage payments (PV) | +€323,600 |
| Ownership costs, 25 years (PV) | +€97,600 |
| Selling costs (PV) | +€11,200 |
| (–) Sale at same real value (PV) | −€278,000 |
| **Net present cost** | **≈ €305,000** |

The cost isn't "zero" despite recovering the real value, for three reasons: the **purchase taxes** (~€59,000), the **25 years of ownership costs** (~€98,000), and the **opportunity cost of the capital** tied up.

## Real cost factoring in historical appreciation

The key: appreciation applies to the **whole home** (€455,000), while she only puts in €91,000 of capital and finances the rest at 3%. Leverage works in her favour.

**Barcelona historical data:** over the last decade prices rose **+61.5% nominal** (~4.9%/yr, ~3% real), more than double inflation. Over the long run, through full cycles (including the 2008–2014 crisis, ~−40%), a prudent central scenario is **~4% nominal (~2% real)**.

| Appreciation (nominal / real) | Sale price in 25 yr | Net present cost |
|---|---|---|
| 2% / 0% — flat real value | €747,000 | ≈ €305,000 (cost) |
| 3% / ~1% — conservative | €953,000 | ≈ €228,000 (cost) |
| **4% / ~2% — central** | **€1.21M** | **≈ €135,000 (cost)** |
| 5% / ~3% — Barcelona's historical pace | €1.54M | ≈ €15,000 (break-even) |
| 6% / ~4% — optimistic | €1.95M | ≈ −€132,000 (net gain) |

**Read:** at Barcelona's actual historical pace (~5% nominal), 25 years of ownership comes out essentially **free** in present-value terms — appreciation covers taxes, interest, and costs. At a more prudent 4%, the real cost is ~€135,000 (vs. €305,000 with no appreciation).

**Two factors that can change it:**

1. **Cycle risk.** The ~5% is an average that includes a ~40% drop (2008–2014). Buying high and selling at a trough would yield much less; 25 years smooths the risk but doesn't eliminate it.
2. **Capital gains tax (IRPF).** The tables assume the **primary-residence exemption** (sale from age 65, or reinvestment in another primary home → €0). If there's **no** exemption, the nominal gain is taxed at ~26–28%: in the central case it adds ~€65,000; at Barcelona pace, ~€90,000–100,000. **Worth confirming the exit plan** (live in it long-term / reinvest): it's worth €60,000–100,000.

**Conclusion:** with no appreciation the real cost is ~€300,000; with Barcelona's historical appreciation the deal approaches break-even or generates a gain. The cheap, leveraged mortgage means even moderate appreciation greatly improves the outcome versus the apparent "cost".

## Buy vs. rent (25 years, present value)

**Renting** is a 100% sunk cost, but avoids purchase taxes, IBI, community fees, and maintenance. For an equivalent 54 m² flat — furnished, high quality, with a terrace, in Dreta de l'Eixample (~€1,300–1,900/month):

| Equivalent rent | PV of 25 years (real, 2% discount) |
|---|---|
| €1,300/mo (low) | ≈ €305,000 |
| €1,600/mo (central) | ≈ €375,000 |
| €1,900/mo (high) | ≈ €445,000 |

**Buying** (from the analysis above): no appreciation ≈ €305,000 cost; central case (2% real, CGT-exempt) ≈ €135,000 cost.

| | Rent low €305k | Rent central €375k | Rent high €445k |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Buy, no appreciation (€305k)** | break-even | buy saves ~€70k | buy saves ~€140k |
| **Buy, central appreciation (€135k)** | buy saves ~€170k | buy saves ~€240k | buy saves ~€310k |

**Read:** buying wins in almost every case, and clearly so once you assume any appreciation. Only the worst case for buying — no appreciation and cheap rent — is a tie.

**Important caveats:**

- **Horizon.** The ~13% purchase taxes only amortize over long holds. Under ~6–7 years, renting wins. This analysis assumes long-term residence (or renting the property out).
- **Return on freed-up capital.** Renting frees ~€150,000 (down payment + taxes). At 2% real it's already captured in the discount rate; if that capital earned more (e.g. equities ~5% real), renting narrows the gap, though the leveraged home usually still wins.
- **Non-financial.** Renting offers flexibility and liquidity; buying concentrates wealth in a single asset and a single city.

## Sensitivity: how do €10,000 more or less in the final price matter?

A €10,000 change in price does **not** change the real cost by €10,000, because most of it is **principal recovered at sale**. Only the tax friction on that amount is truly lost. The impact depends almost entirely on the appreciation assumption:

| Scenario | Δ real present-day cost per ±€10,000 of price |
|---|---|
| No appreciation (flat real value) | ±~€4,500 |
| Central (2% real) | ±~€800 |
| Barcelona's historical pace (~3% real) | ≈ €0 (even marginally favourable) |

**Why so small in the realistic cases** — breaking down the €10,000:

- **Purchase taxes: 13% × €10,000 = €1,300** → lost forever.
- **~€400 extra selling costs** (PV).
- **The €10,000 of principal** is recovered at sale: in full in real terms if value holds or rises; only time-discounted if value stays flat (recovering €10,000 in 25 years equals ~€6,000 today, which is why the no-appreciation case penalizes more).

The **guaranteed, unrecoverable cost of paying €10,000 more is ~€1,300–1,700** (taxes + selling friction). The rest is recovered if the market behaves.

**Negotiation takeaway:**

- If you believe in Barcelona appreciation, €10,000 on the final price is worth **under €1,000** of real cost → don't break the deal over it.
- If skeptical of appreciation, each €10,000 is worth **~€4,000–5,000** of real cost → push harder.
- Tax note: if the gain is taxable (no exemption), a higher purchase price **raises the cost basis** and reduces the future taxable gain, recovering ~26–28% of that €10,000 at sale.

*Caveat:* the "≈ €0 / favourable" result assumes future appreciation is independent of the price paid today. Overpaying does **not** guarantee the same exit price; the honest planning figure is the central one (~€800).
